So you want to make your own train layout / model railroad at home and don’t know where to start, eh?


You’ve read a bunch of magazines, watched a couple videos, and seen all these superb room-filling extensive layouts and then got turned away when they told you it took decades to build them… And then there are all the prototype modelers who go on and on about super-detailing and running operations at slow crawling speeds, and honestly it doesn’t sound that exciting… So you think maybe you should forget being an actual model railroader and instead just play with TrainZ, it’s just so much easier.

If all you want is to run some trains, have fun, like you know, right now, without being too involved, yes you can! Read on for some tips.

1- Introduction

As with any project, I like to start with what this document is about, yet even more importantly I want to point out what this is not about.

Who is this guide not for?

  • Your dream is to create the ultimate museum quality model railroad.
  • You know exactly the model railroad you want and it’s going to be that gigantic multi-level track design.
  • You must have the finest scenery possible with the most excruciating interesting detail.
  • You’re an experienced model railroad builder with years of experience.
  • You're working towards your NMRA Master Builder certification.
  • You already know everything and you’re just reading this to see what you can complain about.
  • You don’t like to read and would rather be watching a YouTube video instead.
  • You don’t like to learn and do stuff, can you just buy the whole thing on Amazon already?

Then skip this. There’s not going to be much or anything useful for you here.

Who is this guide for?

  • You like trains. Trains are fun. They are also, like, you know, toys, right?
  • OK some trains are quite expensive, so maybe not just “kids toys”, but still that’s about playing.
  • Some kind of train running would be cool, but really you don’t know what or how nor care that much.
  • This is just a hobby, right? Limited time and other interestings to do in life.

What we will and what we will not cover here:

  • We’ll discuss layout design: what is good to get started, a few typical mistakes to avoid, but essentially we’ll be designing nothing fancy. We’ll keep things simple.
  • We’ll discuss working with a limited budget. This model railroading thing can be a money sink like many other hobbies. We’ll discuss starting with simple things that are purposely not the most expensive things yet are just good enough to get started, spend money where it matters, and still what are the pros and cons, and where we can go from there.

About me, the writer:

  • I don’t consider myself a serious “model railroader”.
  • I’m a software engineer. I like writing software for fun, playing with computers, yet I also have side hobbies with electronics, woodworking, and such.
  • I like model trains as a hobby.
  • I don’t have a specific railroad or era preference, and rather not have one.
  • I have what I would consider to be the most simple “track layout” at home. Just enough to be good for my own usage, and no more.
  • I am no authority in this subject. I don’t pretend to know everything. I know just enough to get by, and then there’s a ton of things I don’t know.
  • However I love learning new things, getting things done, and sharing what I found.

When I design something, my favorite principle is “keep it simple”. If things seem complicated or tiresome, then what’s the fun? This is a hobby to have fun after all.

Remember the whole point of a hobby is for you to have fun. Building a model railroad requires a lot of skills in a variety of subjects, and you’re not going to excel nor even like them all. So really, focus on what you like. Go in as deep on the parts that you find interesting, and skimp on the other parts you don’t care for, or even better get a friend to do them for you.

2- The Lazy Approach to Limiting Scope Creep

When reading model railroading magazines, I always see depictions of huge, elaborate, basement-filling layouts. Even the simple track plans are depicted with complex advanced sceneries which are way beyond my level of interest and capacity. I’m no artist and honestly I have no interest in spending the next 10-20 years building scenery for a train layout.

Model railroading forums are full of “perfect modelers” who describe their advanced designs, their perfectly weathered cars, their perfect sceneries, and quite frankly I always find that discouraging. I don’t want to spend that much time on what is just a side hobby.

Years ago I decided I wanted to have a small layout at home. Forget the advanced track plans, the gigantic room-filling layouts, the time consuming scenery… is there something I can do that will work for me?

As a matter of fact, yes there is. I have my own fully satisfying layout. It’s perpetually a work in progress. It works. I use it.

But first we must understand what we are looking for. The goal is to limit “scope creep”. Scope creep happens when you try to define a project, and keep modifying your requirements as you go along. This is a definitive way to never be able to start. Another roadblock is having too much choice, or being afraid of making the wrong choices. To avoid that, it’s useful to have some insight of what you want to achieve, and use it to limit your choices, and then at some point if something feels right, just go for it, and -- most important -- allow yourself to fail. Yes, “failure” is an option.

Failing is a big word and lots of people are afraid of failing. But what if instead of failing, you are instead learning? I have torn and rebuilt my home layout 3, err I mean 4, times already, and each time it was because I wanted to explore something different. I got bored with the previous one and wanted something else. This is not a “failure”, it’s a learning curve because I had a good time with the previous layout till I didn’t. And it helps to plan ahead, in these cases I had planned from the start that these layouts would be temporary.

4.2- NCE Command Stations

First I have to tell you there's, in my opinion, no right choice. It mostly depends on your layout size and how much expansion you want in the future.

The NCE system is basically composed of a "main unit" and a number of *optional* accessories.

Main Unit:

The main unit provides the power to the track as well as interpret the DCC commands.

Option 1 (recommended): Power Cab Starter Set.

This is the unit we have on the workbench at Randall.

It can power a small layout (say 4x8) just fine. It serves both as a mainline power AND programming track.

The "Cab" controller _is_ the both power supply, and the DCC command station, and the *wired* throttle you use.

It is a wired system.

Setup is very easy: a power brick supplies power. The throttle connects to the little panel with the 2 jacks. From there 2 wires go to your track. That's it.

Price: Around $200   https://amzn.to/3D2vSfN

System size: This is a 2 Amp unit. You can easily run 2 or 4 engines at the same time with this.

Expansion:

- If you want to use a wireless throttle, you can later add a radio box (NCE RB02, $160) and then use wireless ProCabs ($230) if you want.

NCE RB02: https://tonystrains.com/product/nce-5240023-rb02-916mhz-radio-base-station ~ $160

NCE ProCab *Radio*: https://yankeedabbler.com/all-scales-nce-524-11-deluxe-master-procabr-tm-wireless-dcc-thr ~ $230

Warning: there are 2 versions of the ProCab, one is wired (524-10), and one is wireless (524-11).

- if you want to use wired, you can have multiple panels with the 2 little wired plugs around the layout and move from one place to another using a wired controller.

Option 2 (likely overkill):  Power Pro Starter Set

 or

Price: Around $550 for the wired version https://amzn.to/3z6SQBB

Price: Around $650 for the wireless version

https://trainlife.com/products/ph-pro-r-wireless-5-amp-starter-set?variant=41483167175

This is the unit that powers the main layout at Randall.

The starter set comes with the main "computer" command station, and one ProCab throttle.

Be careful as there are 2 versions of the ProCab: one is wired, and one is wireless.

(you can tell it's a wireless ProCab when you see the little red LED protruding at the top of the unit).

Also the kit does not come with a power supply. It needs to be bought separately.

https://amzn.to/3CZ2p6K $70

System size: This is a 5 Amp unit. You can easily run up to 5-8 engines with that.

There is a 10 Amp version of that that can power a lot more trains. But at that point it's better to use multiple 5 Amp boosters and segment your track in different districts like is done at Randall.

Setup is more complicated: There are 2 outputs, one for mainline and a separated one for a separate programming track.

You can power the track directly, or like at Randall add additional NCE Boosters and Power Districts but that's not required.

Expansion:

- The wired version can be expanded to wireless using an NCE RB02 (radio) later, which allows using ProCab wireless throttles.

Not Recommended

One product I recommend to avoid is the NCE DCC Twin. That's what I have. It's a simplified unit designed to run 2 trains but it's very limited in the way it operates as it can only handle trains on DCC address #3 or #4.  The command panel is very unsophisticated. It’s essentially a watered down version of the Power Cab Starter Set, without the good controller.
